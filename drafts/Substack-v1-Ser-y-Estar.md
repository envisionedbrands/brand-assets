Here it is.

*Once the snobbery is gone and the cost is solved, what is left is not "access to AI." What is left is the question of who you are when the thing you deemed your value — the thing you spent twenty years getting good at, the work that defines what you charge for — gets done by software in eight seconds.*

I want to answer it now. But first, I want to give you a Spanish lesson.

---

## Ser and estar

Spanish has two verbs for "to be."

When I used to teach Spanish, this was the moment students lost their minds — because in English, you only need one word. You just… *are*.

Spanish forces a distinction.

**Ser** is who you are. Permanent. Essential. *Soy María. Soy venezolana. Soy madre.* That is not changing. That is what I am at the core.

**Estar** is how you are. Temporary. Situational. *Estoy cansada. Estoy trabajando. Estoy en Amsterdam.* I am tired. I am working. I am in Amsterdam. But not forever.

Here is the thing: in Spanish, you cannot mix them up without saying something completely different. *Ser aburrido* means you are a boring person — a permanent condition of being. *Estar aburrido* means you are bored right now — a temporary state. Same word in English. Entirely different realities in Spanish.

English collapses that distinction.

And I think that is where the crisis begins.

---

## We used the wrong verb

Most of us have spent years saying things like *"I am a strategist." "I am a designer." "I am a coach." "I am a consultant." "I am the person who does X."*

And we said it with **ser** energy. Like it was permanent. Essential. Who we fundamentally are.

But it was always **estar**.

It was always what we were doing. Not who we were being.

The work we do is a state, not an essence. It is something we are currently occupied by. It is conditional, responsive, situational. It changes when the market changes. It changes when the tools change. It changes when we change.

We just did not have the grammar to notice.

So when the work starts to shift — when AI can approximate the thing we spent twenty years getting good at — we feel like we are losing ourselves.

We are not losing ourselves. We just got the verbs confused.

---

## The identity reckoning no one is naming

Most of the conversation about AI right now is framed as an economic question. *Will it take my job? Will it undercut my prices? Do I need to learn it or will I fall behind?*

Those are real questions. I wrote about them last week.

But underneath the economic anxiety is something older and harder. An identity reckoning the current conversation is not naming, because the people experiencing it do not yet have the language for it.

They call it "pivoting." They call it "rebranding." They call it "burnout." They call it "the dip." They call it "finding my next chapter."

What it actually is: you built your identity *around* what you do. Not who you are. The craft. The deliverable. The role. *"I'm the messaging strategist." "I'm the content coach." "I'm the brand designer."* The work became the person.

And it worked. For years, often decades. Until it did not.

Until the work changed.

Until AI changed it, or the market changed it, or you changed it. And suddenly the thing that told you who you were no longer fits.

That is not a marketing problem. That is not a positioning problem. That is not even really an AI problem.

That is an identity built on estar trying to survive a shift that only ser can survive.

---

## A call I was on, last month

A few weeks ago, I was on a call with one of the sharpest brand strategists working today. She had just spent forty minutes walking me through her framework, her mastermind, her vision. She had articulated exactly what she wanted AI to do for her. She had named that she wanted to become a household name in her niche. Everything was clean. Everything was articulate. Everything was there.

And then, forty-seven minutes in, she said, quietly:

*"I don't know what's possible."*

She was not confused about tactics. She was standing at the edge of who she had been — "the messaging and content person" — and sensing that what she was becoming was bigger than that label had ever allowed.

That gap, between who you were and who you are becoming, is the identity reckoning.

She is not alone. I am having this conversation with founder after founder. The surface topic is AI. The real topic is *who am I now, if I am not the thing I have been for the last ten years?*

And most of us, when we hit that question, do the same thing.

We double down on the doing.

We add more services. We work harder. We stay busier. We take on new clients we do not want. We rebrand around a different deliverable. We keep proving worth through effort, because the alternative — sitting with the identity question — is terrifying.

But the doing is not what is threatened. The doing was always estar.

What is threatened is the story we wrapped around the doing and called "who I am."

## Why AI forces this now

AI did not create the identity reckoning. Every generation of founders, artists, and craftspeople has hit some version of this moment when their tools changed. The photographer when the darkroom disappeared. The copywriter when the internet did. The accountant when the spreadsheet arrived.

What AI did is compress the timeline to zero.

Previous technology shifts gave you a decade to quietly renegotiate your identity. You could tell yourself you were "still really a darkroom photographer, just adapting." The new tool slotted into the old identity without threatening it.

AI does not give you that cushion.

When the tool can approximate in eight seconds what it took you years to learn, there is no "adapting" language that covers the gap. The doing is visible and cheap. What you actually brought — the taste, the judgment, the accumulated context, the way you see — is suddenly the only thing left, and it was the thing you had stopped valuing because the market never asked for it directly.

AI is not threatening your work. AI is holding up a mirror and asking:

*If what you produce can be produced without you, who are you when you are not producing?*

That is the ser question.

Most people have not been asked it. Not once. Not ever. And they have built entire careers specifically so they would not have to sit with it.

> **AI doesn't steal your value. It reveals that you built your identity on estar when you needed ser.**

## I have done this twice

I am not writing this as theory. I have lived it. Twice.

The first time, I was a creative director in a design agency. That was who I was. *Soy creative director.* Said with ser energy. Said as permanent. Said as identity.

Then I moved from design into brand strategy, and the identity broke before the new one had formed. For a year I could not answer the basic question of what I did. "Brand strategist" felt too small. "Designer" was no longer true. Everything in between sounded like I was apologizing for leaving the old world.

I remember specifically sitting at a dinner in Amsterdam, trying to answer *so what do you do?* and hearing myself stitch four half-answers together, watching the person across from me gently disengage. I went home furious. Not with them. With myself. For still using estar when I needed to find ser.

The second time was more recent. AI arrived, and I watched some of my most foundational work — writing, positioning, methodology — become something a tool could do a workable version of in an afternoon. The question came back, sharper this time.

*If AI can do this, who am I?*

The answer I eventually arrived at is the one this essay is about. I was never the work. I just forgot. The writing, the positioning, the methodology are things I *do*. They are estar. They always were. Who I am — the way I see pattern, the questions I ask, the specific angle of attention I bring into a room — that is ser. And no tool is coming for that, because no tool even knows to look.

The identity I had built on estar had to die. The one I now build on ser holds, because it is not anchored to a deliverable. It is anchored to a way of being.

That shift is not available to everyone. It is available to anyone willing to sit with the question most people arrange their entire life around avoiding.

---

## What changes when you make the shift

When you build identity on estar:
- Your worth is measured by output
- Tool shifts feel existential
- Rest feels like losing ground
- You compete on execution
- You scale by doing more
- You are always one market change away from a crisis

When you build identity on ser:
- Your worth is your way of seeing
- Tool shifts are opportunities to extend your reach, not threats to your identity
- Rest is integral — it is where ser gets heard
- You compete on perception, taste, and judgment
- You scale by codifying how you think, not by doing more
- Market changes are interesting, not terrifying

The second list is not a productivity upgrade. It is a different architecture. And the bridge between them is exactly the conversation I keep having with the founders who come to me for "AI strategy" and end up in a very different conversation than the one they booked.

Because the question is never really *how do I use AI?*

The question, always, is *who am I if AI can do this?*

---

## Where I land

The identity reckoning is not a crisis. It is an invitation.

An invitation to stop confusing your work with your worth. To stop performing expertise and start designing from essence. To stop scaling the noise of what you do and start amplifying the signal of who you are.

The founders who thrive in the next era will not be the ones who learned the tools fastest. They will be the ones who were willing to ask the harder question first.

*Who are you when effort no longer proves worth?*

That is the ser question. English does not have the grammar for it. Spanish does, and my grandmother did, and every language that refused to collapse being into doing does.

English collapsed them because it was convenient. Then we built economies on top of that collapse, and careers, and whole identities.

And now the tool has arrived that is going to force the uncollapsing whether we want it or not.

You can let the tool do it violently, by taking the doing before you have found the being.

Or you can do it on your own terms, now, by sitting with the question early and letting the answer form before the ground shifts under it.

*Refusal does not stop the shift. It just removes your voice from it.*

And doubling down on estar does not protect ser. It just delays the reckoning until it costs more.
